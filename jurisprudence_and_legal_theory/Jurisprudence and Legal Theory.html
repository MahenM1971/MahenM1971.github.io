WHAT IS JURISPRUDENCE AND LEGAL THEORY
Jurisprudence and legal theory is the philosophical study and understanding of the
law. It covers the spectrum from legal positivism, to natural law theories, to
everything in between.
JOHN L. AUSTIN AND THE COMMAND THEORY
OF LAW
Austin begins his analysis with the concept of the "signification of desire," or what he terms the "imperative mood." He draws a
distinction between commands and requests, noting that while a person issuing a command has the authority to enforce
compliance through sanctions, a person making a request lacks such enforcement power.
Commands, according to Austin, are further divided into two categories: laws and orders. The key difference between the two
lies in their permanence. Laws possess a standing authority, whereas orders are situational and temporary, arising and
dissipating with specific occasions.
Laws themselves are subdivided into two types: those imposing legal obligations and those imposing moral obligations. As a
legal positivist, Austin rejects the notion of natural law and its inherent morality, focusing instead on the formal structure of legal
systems.
Austin also categorizes laws as either "laws properly so called" or "laws not properly so called." The latter includes rules set by
individuals in positions of authority, such as a parent to a child or a master to a servant. These, he argues, do not qualify as true
laws in the formal sense.
Within the category of "laws properly so called," Austin further distinguishes between "laws strictly so called" and "laws not
strictly so called." The latter encompasses descriptive rules, such as the laws of science, which are not prescriptive in nature.
Finally, Austin asserts that every "law strictly so called" is a command issued by a sovereign authority, backed by the threat of a
sanction for non-compliance. This definition underscores his view that the essence of law lies in its enforceability and its origin in
a sovereign power.
HART’S CRITICISM OF AUSTIN
According to H.L.A. Hart, not every law "strictly so called" can be reduced to a command issued by a sovereign and enforced by
a sanction. Hart challenges Austin's command theory by highlighting the distinct nature of civil law, which governs rights and
obligations. Unlike criminal law, where enforcement is often carried out by the state (or the Crown), civil law relies on the mutual
recognition of residual rights, which are typically established by constitutional or statutory provisions. In this framework,
individuals enforce their rights through legal processes rather than through direct state-imposed sanctions.
Hart also critiques Austin's conception of sovereignty. While Austin defines sovereignty as a situation where a population
habitually obeys a common determinate superior—who, in turn, is not in the habit of obeying any other superior—Hart argues
that this model fails to account for the complexities of modern legal systems. For instance, Hart points out that the concept of
habitual obedience breaks down during transitions of power, such as when a monarchy changes. Even if the Crown is viewed as
a continuous institution, it is fundamentally different from a sovereign legislature, which is composed of democratically elected
officials. These officials are not only accountable to the electorate but are also bound by the same constitutional and
administrative laws they help create and uphold.
In Hart's view, Austin's theory oversimplifies the nature of law by ignoring the role of constitutional frameworks, the rule of law,
and the democratic accountability of law-making bodies. Hart's critique emphasizes that law is not merely about commands and
sanctions but also about rules that facilitate social cooperation, define rights, and limit the powers of those who govern.
H. L. A. HART AND THE CONCEPT OF LAW
H.L.A. Hart draws a clear distinction between social habits and social rules, emphasizing that the difference lies in the internal and external perceptions of those who follow them. Social habits are
behaviors that occur regularly without conscious thought or rationale, whereas social rules require individuals to conform with an understanding of the rule's purpose and significance. In other words, social
rules involve a conscious acceptance of the rule as a standard of behavior, accompanied by a sense of obligation.
Hart categorizes social rules into two types: rules enforcing legal obligation and rules enforcing moral obligation. Within the realm of legal rules, he further divides them into primary rules and secondary
rules.
Primary rules are those that directly govern behavior, telling individuals what they may or may not do. These rules impose duties and obligations, such as prohibitions against theft or violence.
Secondary rules, on the other hand, are rules about rules. They provide the framework for creating, modifying, and interpreting primary rules. Hart identifies three types of secondary rules:
1. Rules of recognition: These rules identify what counts as law within a legal system. They provide criteria for determining the validity of legal rules, such as constitutional provisions or legislative
enactments.
2. Rules of change: These rules allow for the creation, amendment, or repeal of primary rules. They establish procedures for how laws can be modified over time.
3. Rules of adjudication: These rules govern the process of interpreting and applying the law, typically through courts or other judicial bodies. They also include mechanisms for resolving disputes and
enforcing legal decisions.
Hart also introduces the concept of the ultimate rule of recognition, which serves as the foundational criterion for identifying valid laws within a legal system. In the context of modern legal systems, Hart
illustrates this concept with the example of the King in Parliament in the United Kingdom. This principle signifies that the laws enacted by Parliament, with the formal approval of the monarch, are recognized
as the highest source of legal authority. The ultimate rule of recognition thus provides the basis for the validity of all other rules within the legal system.
By distinguishing between primary and secondary rules, Hart offers a more nuanced understanding of law than Austin's command theory. He highlights the dynamic and structured nature of legal systems,
where secondary rules enable the creation, adaptation, and enforcement of primary rules, ensuring the continuity and functionality of the legal order.
HART’S MINIMUM CONTENT OF NATURAL LAW
According to H.L.A. Hart, the legality of positive law is not dependent on its morality; in other words, a law can be legally valid
and binding even if it is morally unjust or objectionable. This separation of law and morality is a central tenet of Hart's legal
positivism, which insists that the validity of law is determined by its source (such as legislative enactment or judicial decision)
rather than its moral content. For example, an unjust or immoral law can still be considered legally valid if it has been properly
enacted within the legal system.
However, Hart acknowledges that there is a relationship between law and morality in practice. He recognizes that the positive
law of a state often reflects the morality of the people who constitute that society. This is because laws are typically shaped by
the values, beliefs, and social norms of the community. Hart refers to this as the minimum content of natural law, a concept he
introduces in The Concept of Law. He argues that, in order to survive and function, any legal system must incorporate certain
basic principles that align with human nature and the realities of social life. These principles include rules against violence, theft,
and deception, as well as mechanisms for enforcing agreements and protecting individuals.
In this way, Hart allows for a minimal overlap between law and morality, not as a matter of necessity for legal validity, but as a
practical reflection of the shared values and needs of society. This "minimum content" ensures that the law serves its
fundamental purpose of maintaining order and facilitating cooperation, even as Hart maintains the positivist stance that law and
morality are conceptually distinct.
SAINT THOMAS AQUINAS AND THE SUMMA
THEOLOGICA
In the Summa Theologica, Saint Thomas Aquinas identifies and elaborates on four categories of law, each serving a distinct purpose within his theological and
philosophical framework:
1. Eternal Law: This is the ultimate and overarching law that governs the entire universe. It is the divine reason and wisdom of God, which orders all creation and
sustains the natural and moral order. Eternal law is known fully only to God, as it encompasses the divine plan for the cosmos, including life, death, and the
fundamental principles of existence.
2. Natural Law: Aquinas describes natural law as the participation of rational creatures (humans) in the eternal law. It is the moral and ethical principles that are
inherent in human nature and accessible through reason. Natural law guides human behavior by reflecting the divine order and providing a foundation for
distinguishing right from wrong. It is universal and unchanging, rooted in the nature of creation itself.
3. Human Law (Positive Law): This refers to the laws created by human societies to regulate behavior and maintain order. Human law is derived from natural law
but is shaped by the specific needs, customs, and circumstances of a particular community. It represents humanity's attempt to interpret and apply the principles
of natural law in practical, real-world situations. While human law should align with natural law, it is subject to change and variation depending on the context.
4. Divine Law: Divine law is the law revealed by God through scripture and religious teachings. It provides guidance for humanity's ultimate purpose: salvation and
union with God. Unlike natural law, which is accessible through reason, divine law is known through revelation and is necessary for addressing matters beyond
human understanding, such as the path to eternal life and spiritual redemption.
Aquinas's framework integrates these four types of law into a coherent system, emphasizing the harmony between divine wisdom, human reason, and the moral order.
While eternal and divine law originate from God, natural law bridges the divine and human realms, and human law represents the practical application of these principles
in societal governance. Together, they reflect Aquinas's belief in a rational and ordered universe guided by divine providence.
LON LUVOIS FULLER AND THE MORALITY OF
THE LAW
Lon L. Fuller proposed a theory that bridges the gap between positive law and natural
law by emphasizing the morality of law. He argued that law should not be understood
solely through a subjective interpretation of positive law (which focuses on the formal
rules and procedures created by human authorities) or an objective interpretation of
natural law (which emphasizes universal moral principles). Instead, Fuller believed that
the true morality of law lies in the middle ground, where compromise and balance are
achieved between these two perspectives.
Fuller stressed that equality and justice are fundamental to human coexistence and that
these principles can only be realized through a legal system that embodies both
procedural and substantive morality. For Fuller, the morality of law is not just about the
content of the rules but also about the processes by which those rules are created,
interpreted, and applied.
JOHN FINNIS AND THE NATURAL LAW
TRADITION
Enlightenment philosopher David Hume argued that an "ought" (a moral or prescriptive statement)
cannot be derived from an "is" (a factual or descriptive statement). This principle, known as Hume's
Guillotine, highlights the logical gap between facts and values, asserting that moral obligations cannot
be inferred solely from observations of the world. Hume's critique challenges the idea that morality can
be grounded in natural facts alone, emphasizing the need for a separate normative framework to justify
ethical conclusions.
John Finnis, a contemporary natural law theorist, responded by acknowledging that the "ought" is not
directly derived from the "is." However, he argued that the validity of what is—existing social, legal, and
moral systems—is often the result of past reasoning about what ought to be. Finnis contended that
human practical reasoning, guided by basic goods such as life, knowledge, and justice, shapes societal
norms and institutions over time. In this way, the "ought" influences the "is," as moral and practical
decisions become embedded in the fabric of reality. Finnis thus bridges the gap between fact and value,
showing how moral reasoning historically informs and justifies the structures of human society.
MARX MARXISM AND MARXIST LEGAL THEORY
According to Karl Marx, the economics of law plays a central role in shaping legal systems. He argued that
the laws of a society, their interpretation, and their implementation are fundamentally determined by the
economic structure and the interests of the ruling class. Marx believed that the might of wealth enables the
ruling class—the bourgeoisie—to maintain its dominance, creating a division between the ruling elite and the
working class, or proletariat. For Marx, the legal system is not a neutral arbiter of justice but a tool used by the
ruling class to perpetuate its power and control over the means of production.
Marx saw the unity of the common man—the masses or proletariat—as the key to achieving a social
revolution, which would in turn make a legal revolution possible. By uniting and overthrowing the bourgeoisie,
the proletariat could dismantle the existing legal and economic structures and replace them with a system that
serves the interests of the majority. However, Marx also warned that such a revolution could lead to the
dictatorship of the proletariat, replacing the dictatorship of the bourgeoisie. To avoid this, he emphasized the
need for committed leadership dedicated to the ideals of equality, justice, and collective well-being. Without
such leadership, the revolution risked becoming another form of oppression. Thus, for Marx, true social,
economic, political, and legal reform requires not only the unity of the masses but also visionary leaders who
remain steadfast to the principles that make such transformative change possible.
KARL FRIEDRICH VON SAVIGNY, FRIEDRICH
CARL VON SAVIGNY AND THE VOLKSGEIST
Karl Friedrich von Savigny introduced the concept of Volksgeist, or the "spirit of the people," as
the driving force behind the development of human society. He argued that the customs,
traditions, and collective consciousness of a community shape its institutions, culture, and legal
systems. This idea emphasized that law is not an abstract construct imposed from above but an
organic outgrowth of the lived experiences and values of a people.
His son, Friedrich Carl von Savigny, extended this reasoning to the realm of law, asserting that
laws are not made but found. According to him, laws emerge naturally from the needs and
circumstances of society, validated by the Volksgeist—the spirit of the people. In this view, the
legal system reflects the collective identity and evolving requirements of the community. If law is
the corpus (body) of human society, the Volksgeist is its spirit, animating and guiding its
development. This perspective highlights the deep connection between law and culture,
emphasizing that legal systems are rooted in the historical and social context of the people they
serve.
HANS KELSEN AND THE PURE THEORY OF LAW
In The Pure Theory of Law and The General Theory of Law and State, Hans Kelsen argued that any moral interpretation of the law, or any notion of what the law ought
to be, introduces an impurity that contaminates the law's pure essence. Kelsen sought to separate law from morality, focusing instead on its formal structure and validity.
Central to Kelsen's theory is the concept of the Grundnorm, or "basic norm," which serves as the foundational principle that validates the legal system. According to
Kelsen, the Grundnorm derives its authority from the collective acceptance of the law as valid. It is not a moral or ethical principle but a presupposition that underpins the
entire legal order, providing a framework for the creation and enforcement of legal norms.
Kelsen also contended that law is not directed at individuals in a direct sense. Instead, it empowers officials to act when legal norms are violated. For example, when an
individual commits a delict (a violation of the law), the first norm—the prohibition of the act—triggers the enforcement of the second norm, which is the sanction imposed
by the state. In this way, the legal system operates as a hierarchical structure of norms, with the Grundnorm at its base.
Critics of Kelsen argue that his theory oversimplifies the nature of law. They contend that his focus on the coercive aspect of law neglects the positive obligations that
legal systems enforce, such as the protection of civil liberties or the regulation of contractual relationships under civil law. Additionally, Kelsen's theory has been
criticized for its inability to account for the dynamic and often contested nature of legal norms in diverse societies.
A further critique centers on the Grundnorm itself. While Kelsen posits that the Grundnorm presupposes the validity and enforceability of a state's constitution, this
assumption assumes a universal acceptance that may not exist in reality. Critics point out that democratic minorities or marginalized groups may not share the same
presupposition about the legal system's validity. This raises questions about whether the Grundnorm can truly be a stable or universally recognized foundation for law.
Moreover, the Grundnorm's meaning may vary among individuals or groups, creating ambiguity and leaving jurists unable to definitively discern its content or application.
RICHARD DAWKINS AND LAW’S EMPIRE
In Law’s Empire, Ronald Dworkin argues that the validity of the law depends on its interpretation, and that any interpretation
must maintain the law’s integrity. For Dworkin, law is not merely a set of rules but an interpretive practice that seeks to reconcile
legal principles with moral and political values.
To illustrate his theory, Dworkin introduces the hypothetical figure of Judge Hercules, an idealized judge with exceptional wisdom
and insight. Judge Hercules is capable of distinguishing between "arguments of fit" and "arguments of substance." Arguments of
fit are grounded in how well an interpretation aligns with existing legal precedents and institutional history, while arguments of
substance involve moral and political principles that justify the law. By balancing these two types of arguments, Hercules aims to
arrive at the most coherent and principled interpretation of the law.
Dworkin’s theory culminates in the "one right answer" thesis, which asserts that every legal question has a single correct answer,
even if it is not always obvious or easy to discern. This thesis stems from his belief that law has an underlying unity and a true
meaning that can be uncovered through rigorous interpretation. However, critics argue that this approach is problematic. By
insisting on one right answer, Dworkin’s theory appears to acknowledge alternative interpretations only to dismiss them,
potentially overlooking the plurality of perspectives and the complexity inherent in legal disputes. This raises questions about
whether the "one right answer" thesis can adequately account for the diversity of legal and moral reasoning in practice.
